Theoretical Anchors: Doctorow & Brock

Our analytical framework is grounded in two key axioms of media studies: Cory Doctorow's theory of Enshittification and Andr√© Brock's work on Platform Vernaculars.

Cory Doctorow: The Enshittification Cycle 1

  • Definition: Platforms progressively degrade their service quality to extract more value, first from users, then business customers, finally for shareholders. This is a predictable decay model.
  • Core Axiom: Once users are locked in, the platform shifts its loyalty from user experience to profit maximization.

"Here is how platforms die: First, they are good to their users; then they abuse their users to make things better for their business customers; finally, they abuse those business customers to claw back all the value for themselves. Then, they die."

‚Äî Cory Doctorow, "The 'Enshittification' of TikTok" (WIRED, 2023)2

Andr√© Brock: Platform Vernaculars & Signifyin' 3

  • Definition: Communities develop unique cultural practices, styles, and communication methods native to a specific platform. These are not merely adaptations, but active shapings of the platform's social space.
  • Core Axiom: Marginalized communities, like Black Twitter, actively resignify platform affordances (e.g., hashtags) to create powerful cultural and political discourse.

"Black Twitter shattered deficit models of Black digital expertise through discourse, affordances, and networked culture. The disembodiment inherent to digital spaces accrues to Black virtuality as resignification..."

‚Äî Andr√© Brock, "The Illumination of Black Twitter" (Media Theory, 2024)4

Platform Vernaculars: Shaping the Digital Conversation

Every major platform, from Instagram to YouTube, cultivates or has cultivated a unique vernacular. These are the organic cultural forms, communication styles, and meme formats that emerge from user interaction with the platform's technical affordances.

The Black Twitter Morphism: Hashtags as Cultural Tools

  • Observation: Black Twitter emerged as a powerful subculture, actively shaping trending topics and discourse.
  • Operation of the Hashtag: Brock identifies the hashtag as serving "triple duty as 'signifier,' 'sign,' and 'signified,' marking as it does the concept to be signified, the cultural context within which the tweet should be understood, and the 'call' awaiting a response." 5 This is a prime example of a cultural re-purposing of a technical feature.
  • Impact: It allowed a cultural group to surface its own discourse, often "unconcerned with the mainstream gaze." 6

"This obviously and intrinsically black tradition has been adopted by a community like Twitter that is, frankly, disproportionately not black."

‚Äî Andr√© Brock, "From the Blackhand Side: Twitter as a Cultural Conversation" (2012)7

From User-Driven to Platform-Driven Feeds

  • Early Stages: Platforms (e.g., early YouTube vlogs, Instagram photo sharing) thrive when user-generated content and vernaculars are prioritized.
  • Shift: As platforms grow, the profit motive leads to suppression or co-option of organic content. The feed algorithm changes to favor sponsored or "engaging" content over genuine social interaction.
  • Example: Early Facebook was about friends; later, it became "cramming our feeds with posts from media outlets and brands." 8

The Enshittification Lifecycle: How Platforms Degrade

Doctorow's model of platform decay describes a three-stage value extraction process that leads to "enshittification":

  • Stage 1: Courtship Phase (Good to Users)
    • Platform offers a superior service, often at a loss, to attract a critical mass of users.
    • Goal: Build network effects, get users locked in.
    • Example: Early TikTok with its "magically good recommendation algorithm" for organic content. 9
  • Stage 2: Extraction Phase (Abuse Users for Business Customers)
    • Once users are locked in, the platform shifts surplus value to business customers (advertisers, corporate partners).
    • Goal: Monetize the captive audience by making it attractive for businesses.
    • Example: Amazon charging third-party sellers increasing fees and ad costs. 10
  • Stage 3: Decay Phase (Abuse Business Customers for Self)
    • Finally, the platform extracts all remaining value for itself and its shareholders, even if it degrades service for businesses and users.
    • Result: The platform "becomes a useless pile of shit." 11
    • Example: Search results flooded with ads, feeds with sponsored content, algorithms working against user interests.

Case Study: TikTok and the "Heating" Mechanism

TikTok serves as a prime example of Doctorow's enshittification in progress. Its initial success relied on an incredibly effective "For You" page algorithm that delighted users. However, this power was soon redirected.

The "Heating" Operation

  • Discovery: A Forbes investigation revealed TikTok's secret "heating button," allowing employees to artificially boost specific videos to millions of users. 12
  • Purpose: This covert operation was used to court influencers and brands, granting them instant viral hits and luring them onto the platform.
  • Doctorow's Metaphor: "TikTok is handing out giant teddy bears" to initially attract partners. 13 However, this free attention is not sustainable for the platform.

"TikTok is not in the business of giving away giant teddy bears. TikTok is only going to funnel free attention to the people it wants to entrap until they are entrapped, then it will withdraw that attention and begin to monetize it."

‚Äî Cory Doctorow, "The 'Enshittification' of TikTok" (WIRED, 2023)14

Consequences for Creators and Users

  • Withdrawal of Attention: Once creators are locked in, TikTok can "punish them by failing to deliver their videos to the users who subscribed to them." 15
  • Algorithmic Shift: The feed is tuned to serve TikTok's interests (ad revenue) over user preferences. Every time a user sees a desired video, TikTok "loses a chance to show you a video it wants you to see." 16
  • Enshittification Confirmed: Doctorow concludes, "The enshittification has begun." 17

Resistance & Adaptation: Communities Push Back

When platforms enter the decay phase, communities don't passively accept degradation. They develop various resistance strategies and adaptive vernaculars.

Strategies of Adaptation and Migration

  • Platform Migration: Users may move to alternative platforms (e.g., Twitter users to Mastodon/Bluesky). However, network effects create significant lock-in, making mass migration challenging. "You may love your friends, but half the time you can‚Äôt agree on what movie to see... Forget it." 18
  • Internal Workarounds: Communities devise new tactics within existing platforms:
    • Posting full content on Facebook to bypass throttled links.
    • Using Instagram "carousel photo dumps" to gain algorithmic boosts.
    • Employing coded language (e.g., "unalive" on TikTok) to evade content moderation.

Networked Protest and State Manipulation (Zeynep Tufekci)

Tufekci's work introduces the capacity vs. signal olog: protests are a "signal" of an underlying capacity to effect change. 19 Social media empowers movements but also introduces vulnerabilities.

  • Power of Coordination: During the Arab Spring, activists used platforms to coordinate, disseminate narratives, and even bypass government internet blackouts. 20 This exemplifies communities seizing control of the information feed.
  • Fragility & Countermeasures: Platforms' advantages (speed, scale) come with weaknesses (surveillance, misinformation). Governments learn to exploit these through "censorship through distraction" ‚Äì flooding platforms with irrelevant or misleading information to "muddy the online waters." 21, 22

Ethics of State Tactics

ZunZuneo: The "Cuban Twitter" Plot (Platform Colonization)

The ZunZuneo project, dubbed "Cuban Twitter," serves as a stark example of deliberate platform creation for political manipulation ‚Äì a form of platform colonization.

Astroturfing Architecture 23

  • Covert Operation: Created by the U.S. government (USAID) in 2010, ZunZuneo was offered as a "free text service" to Cubans, attracting 40,000 users. Its ties to the U.S. government were obscured through offshore companies. 24, 25
  • Phased Content Injection:
    1. Initially, "non-controversial content" (sports, music) built a user base.
    2. The plan was to "later introduce political messages through social bots to encourage dissent." 23
  • Data Collection: The platform also gathered intelligence on users' "political leanings" and "openness." 24

"The developers aimed to use 'non-controversial content,' such as sports and music, to build up subscribers and then introduce political messages through social bots to encourage dissent in an astroturfing initiative."

‚Äî Wikipedia: ZunZuneo (2014) / Associated Press investigation23

Control of Platform = Control of Narrative

  • Axiom: This episode demonstrates that direct control over a platform (infrastructure, content flow) translates directly into control over a community's narrative and potential for behavioral influence.
  • Outcome: ZunZuneo was discontinued in 2012 due to funding, but it highlights the powerful prize of "owning the feed" for geopolitical actors, not just corporations. 23, 26

Platform as Proxy State

The Rise of AI ‚ÄúSlop‚Äù: New Phase of Enshittification

The proliferation of generative AI tools (2023-2025) has introduced a new, accelerated phase of platform degradation. John Oliver describes this deluge as "AI slop" ‚Äì cheap, algorithmically-produced content flooding our feeds. 27

Characteristics of AI Slop

  • Mass Production: AI tools make it "very easy to mass produce and flood social media sites with cheap spam, misinformation, and otherwise empty, low-quality digital stuff." 28
  • Engagement Feedback Loops: AI generates content, bots comment, and real people struggle to differentiate. 28 Platforms actively inject algorithmic content, allowing slop to "sneak in without your permission." 29
  • Corrosion of Reality: Oliver warns that AI slop is "worryingly corrosive to the general concept of objective reality." The mere existence of convincing fakes empowers bad actors to dismiss real content as fabricated (the "liar's dividend"). 30

"AI slop can be somewhat lucrative for its creators, massively lucrative for the platforms that use it to drive engagement, and worryingly corrosive to the general concept of objective reality."

‚Äî John Oliver, "AI Slop" segment, Last Week Tonight (June 2025)30

Implications for Creativity & Revenue

  • Creator Displacement: Platforms may favor AI-generated content over human creators because it's cheap and abundant, displacing artists (e.g., macram√© artist case). 31
  • "Volume Game": AI slop operates as a "volume game, like any form of spam," with "slop influencers" teaching others how to monetize this low-quality content. 32

Italian Brainrot: A Vernacular of Machine Delirium

Italian Brainrot is a recent memetic trend that embodies the peculiar new platform vernacular emerging from the age of AI slop. It's characterized by absurd, AI-generated animal characters with pseudo-Italian names and nonsensical voiceovers, rapidly spreading across TikTok and Instagram. 3, 9, 33

The Feedback Loop of Absurdity

  • Gen Z/Alpha Appeal: Popular among younger generations "because it's stupid, funny, and veeeery addictive." 3 The term "brain rot" itself acknowledges the stupefying effect of mindless scrolling through AI-generated content. 34
  • Algorithmic Amplification: TikTok's algorithm boosts bizarre AI videos due to high "WTF" engagement, creating a feedback loop where more such content floods the feed. 3, 35
  • Ephemeral but Real: While fleeting "internet junk," these memes become genuine cultural artifacts for participants, spawning lore, in-jokes, and even real-world commercialization (Roblox games, merchandise). 36

"In a time when the memetic universe is becoming increasingly fragmented... Italian Brainrot stands out as a rare case of a truly mainstream meme. It's deeply rooted in internet culture with its taste for nonsense, absurdity and political incorrectness."

‚Äî Valentina Tanni, quoted in Network Cultures (2025)3

Machine Delirium & The Polycrisis

  • Symptomatic Response: Some analyses suggest Brainrot memes are products of "machinic delirium" and serve as an "empathic symbol of entropy" in response to the "unfolding 'polycrisis.'" 37
  • New Vernacular of Resistance? Is it genuine cultural resistance against overwhelming digital noise, or merely a symptom of cognitive overload and a descent into shared digital absurdity? This remains an open question.

Who Owns the Feed? A Tug-of-War Olog

The central question of "who owns the feed" reveals a continuous power dynamic between platform owners and user communities. It's not a simple binary, but a complex olog of contested control.

Platform Owners: The Algorithmic Grip

  • Infrastructure Control: Platforms set the rules, design the algorithms, and ultimately shape what content is seen. They assert this control to serve profit motives.
  • Examples: Amazon's ad-filled search results, TikTok's "heating," Meta's injection of recommended content ("we, not you, decide what appears on your feed"). 38
  • AI's Role: AI slop grants platforms a new tool to fill feeds without relying on human creators, raising the specter of a "dystopian internet" of bot-generated content consumed by passive humans. 39

User Communities: Cultural Leverage & Resistance

  • Content & Vitality: Platforms depend on user-generated content and social energy to thrive. This provides communities with leverage.
  • Vernacular Power: Black Twitter's cultural cachet demonstrated how communities can make a platform compelling, forcing acknowledgement.
  • Meaning-Making: Communities still determine what is "cool" or "meaningful," as seen with Italian Brainrot. Algorithms can amplify, but not always create genuine cultural resonance.
  • Active Resistance: From migration to workarounds and direct protest, communities find ways to adapt, subvert, or abandon platforms when enshittification becomes unbearable.

The Future of the Feed: An Unfolding Olog

The balance of power is shifting, increasingly favoring platforms due to lock-in and the rise of AI. However, understanding this enshittification model and recognizing collective user power is crucial.

  • Optimistic Axiom: When platforms degrade too far, users will seek or build new, more open spaces where new cultures can flourish.
  • Pessimistic Axiom: All large platforms may converge on the same extractive model, leading to an ever more manipulated, "weird" feed that real communities struggle to reclaim.
